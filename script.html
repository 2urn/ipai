Framing statement + speaker introductions

6:20 PM ~10 min
Moderator says
Good evening. Welcome to Skills — Oolite Arts' ongoing series on the practical realities of creative practice.

Tonight we're talking about intellectual property in the age of AI. And I want to start by being honest about something: this is not a reassuring conversation. The law is moving — but slowly. The technology is moving fast. And a lot of what you've been told about protecting your work is either incomplete, out of date, or doesn't account for the specific vulnerabilities that artists face right now.

Four months ago I moderated a version of this conversation at the Ratcliffe Art + Design Incubator at FIU with the same two attorneys. A lot has changed since then — and the most important thing that changed is that the Supreme Court declined to weigh in on AI and copyright. Which sounds like nothing happened. In fact, it's one of the most consequential non-decisions creatives need to understand.

Before we get into that, let me introduce our two panelists — both of whom you may remember from February, and both of whom have agreed to help us navigate what is genuinely a chaotic legal landscape with honesty rather than false comfort.

02

The opening scenario: Benedict Cork

6:30 PM ~5 min
Moderator says
I want to open with a story that Somara shared with me, because I think it captures everything we're going to talk about tonight more viscerally than any legal concept can.

In late February, a British singer-songwriter named Benedict Cork posted a snippet of himself playing an unfinished song called "Something Kinda Strange" on TikTok. It got over 100,000 plays. A few days later, people started texting him to congratulate him on the release. Which was strange — because he hadn't finished the song. Eventually, someone sent him the track. It was on streaming platforms under someone else's name. The AI-completed version had a full second verse, a bridge, and lyrics that Cork — his words — "begrudgingly wished he had written."

He got the song removed after a few days. But here's what's important: he had almost no immediate legal recourse. The law wasn't fast enough. And Benedict Cork is one of the lucky ones — he found out. Most artists don't.

I'm going to let that sit for a moment, and then I want to ask both of you: what does a story like this tell us about where IP law actually is right now?
Both panelists — open response
When you hear the Benedict Cork story, what's the most important legal reality it reveals for independent artists?
Opens the conversation viscerally, establishes the speed problem, invites both attorneys to set the tone. Let them react genuinely — don't rush.
If needed, follow up with
  • Is this copyright infringement? Likeness theft? Identity fraud? Or something the law doesn't have a category for yet?
  • If you were Benedict Cork's attorney the day he found out, what's the first call you make?
03

Terms round: five concepts everyone needs

6:37 PM ~12 min ~2 min per term
Moderator says
Before we go further, I want to make sure everyone in the room is working from the same vocabulary. I'm going to give a plain-language definition of five terms — and I'm asking Somara and Andrej to correct me, argue with me, or tell me what I'm missing. These are starting points, not legal definitions. Ready?
Format note: Read your definition, then point to one attorney for the correction/expansion. Keep each to about 90 seconds total. The goal is productive disagreement, not a lecture.
01 · Denial of certiorari
"You say:" On March 2nd, the Supreme Court declined to hear the Thaler case — the case asking whether an AI system could be a copyright author. A lot of headlines said 'Supreme Court rules AI can't hold copyright.' But that's not what happened. The Court didn't rule on anything. They just said 'not this case, not right now.' The lower court decision stands — but this is not settled law from the Supreme Court. Is that right?
→ Andrej What does denial of cert actually mean for how courts across the country treat this issue going forward?
02 · Human authorship requirement
"You say:" US copyright law requires a human author. The Copyright Office confirmed this in 2025 — prompts alone are not sufficient to claim authorship. You have to show meaningful human creative contribution. The question nobody has answered yet is: how much is enough?
→ Somara For an artist in this room tonight who uses AI tools in their work — what does "sufficient human contribution" actually look like in practice? Is there a rule of thumb?
03 · Fair use
"You say:" Fair use is the legal doctrine that allows limited use of copyrighted work without permission. It's evaluated case by case on four factors. Courts are currently split on whether training AI on copyrighted works qualifies as fair use — some courts have said yes, it's transformative; others have said no. This is the most active legal battleground in AI and IP right now.
→ Both If I'm an artist and I find out my work was used to train an AI model — what's my actual legal position today?
04 · Epistemic capture
"You say:" This one's mine — it's not a legal term yet. When I use an AI tool, I'm not just getting outputs. I'm submitting my creative decisions, my prompts, my iterations, my way of thinking about a problem. That thinking process may become training data for the platform. My methodology — not just my work — can be extracted, modeled, and redistributed. I'm calling this epistemic capture. Does the law have anything to say about it?
→ Somara Is there any existing legal framework — trade secret, privacy law, anything — that gets close to protecting how someone thinks and works, not just what they produce?
05 · Right of publicity
"You say:" The right of publicity is a state-level right — not federal — that protects your name, voice, and likeness from unauthorized commercial use. Florida has one. But it's not the same in every state, and the AI voice cloning situation that Benedict Cork experienced may or may not be covered depending on how the law is interpreted.
→ Andrej For Florida-based artists specifically — what does our state's right of publicity actually protect, and what does it miss?
04

Main conversation: four subject areas

6:49 PM ~40 min ~10 min per area
Moderator note: These four areas map to the four guides in the handout. You don't have to name them — just move through the terrain. Each area has a primary question and two follow-ups in case the conversation stalls. You won't need all of them.
Area A · The individual artist (6:49)
→ Somara
An artist in this room tonight is building a practice — they're using Midjourney, or Suno, or ChatGPT in their workflow. They post work online. They have an audience. What are the two or three things they are most urgently getting wrong from a legal standpoint — things that are creating real vulnerability right now, today?
Opens the practical register. Somara's practice is directly about this audience. Let her run.
Follow-up if needed
  • Is copyright registration something every artist should be doing before they publish anything — even a sketch? What's the actual protection it provides?
  • What does a Terms of Service actually say about who owns what — and do artists read them?
→ Andrej
The Benedict Cork story isn't really about copyright in the traditional sense. Someone took a partial performance, completed it with AI, and published it — faster than any legal process could respond. Is this a law problem, a platform problem, or both? And what should artists understand about the gap between what the law promises and what it can actually deliver at speed?
Introduces the speed problem and forces an honest reckoning with legal limits.
→ Both
Here's what strikes me about the Cork story beyond the legal question. Benedict Cork spent years developing a sound — a style, a way of approaching a song. That's his moat. The thing that made him recognizable enough that 100,000 people wanted to hear the rest of the song. And AI collapsed that moat in the time it takes to run a generator. We talk about IP protection as though the goal is to defend a finished work — but what about the accumulated creative identity that made the work valuable in the first place? Is there any legal framework that protects that? Or are we at the point where an artist's moat is no longer their style — it's something else entirely?
Introduces the IP moat collapse in terms the audience feels personally — not as a startup concept but as a creative identity question. Forces both attorneys to say honestly what law can and cannot do here.
Follow-up if needed
  • Venture capitalists are now telling founders that first-mover advantage is no longer enough — that the technology that accelerates your innovation also compresses your differentiation. Is that true for artists too? And if the moat isn't your style anymore, what is it?
Area B · Freelancers, teams, and disclosure (7:00)
→ Andrej
Let's talk about the world most people in this room actually work in — freelance contracts, team projects, client work. If I'm a designer and I use AI in a deliverable I'm submitting to a client, what are my legal obligations around disclosure? And what happens if I don't disclose and the client later finds out?
Andrej's contracts expertise is exactly right here. This is the most immediately actionable area for the audience.
Follow-up if needed
  • What should a freelance contract say right now — in 2026 — about AI use that most contracts don't say yet?
  • If a client delivers a brief that says "no AI" — and a freelancer uses it anyway — is that breach of contract? What's the exposure?
→ Somara
There's a liability cascade I want to get your reaction to: if a freelancer uses AI without disclosing it, the client may receive work with no valid copyright — and may then distribute or sell that work believing they own it. Walk us through what that chain of liability actually looks like. Who gets hurt, and how?
Makes the abstract liability concept concrete. Somara can walk through a real scenario.
Area C · The bigger picture — what even large companies can't protect (7:10)
→ Both
I want to raise something that reframes the entire conversation. Recently, Anthropic — one of the largest AI companies in the world, the maker of Claude — had their code repository exposed. Before their legal team could execute copyright takedown notices, the code had been refactored in multiple languages and released for free online. A company with every legal resource available couldn't protect its core asset. If that's possible — if the speed of AI distribution outpaces the speed of legal protection even for a billion-dollar company — what is the realistic strategy for an individual artist or a small studio?
This is the philosophical heart of the panel. The honest answer is that legal protection is increasingly insufficient — and the conversation needs to go there.
Follow-up if needed
  • Is the answer to stop thinking about IP protection and start thinking about something else — relationship, context, irreducibility? Or is that surrendering too much?
  • Are there legal strategies — trade secrets, licensing, collective action — that actually do work at the speed that AI operates?
→ Andrej
The DMCA was designed around a specific assumption — that content spreads slower than legal process can move. That assumption is now broken. The Anthropic scenario isn't an edge case — it's a stress test that the entire architecture of IP protection failed. So I want to ask you something direct: is the DMCA fixable for the AI era, or is it a category error? Are we trying to use a tool designed for a slower world to solve a problem that operates at a fundamentally different speed?
Moves from the specific Anthropic example to the structural argument. Forces an honest answer about whether legal frameworks are reformable or obsolete. Either answer is interesting.
Follow-up if needed
  • If the answer is that law is always reactive and always will be — what's the non-legal strategy? What do artists actually do when the architecture designed to protect them structurally cannot keep up?
Area D · Institutions, education, and preparing the next generation (7:22)
→ Andrej
You teach law and you work within a university. What should creative programs — art schools, design programs, incubators like RADI — be teaching students about IP and AI right now that most of them aren't? Not the plagiarism conversation. The deeper one. What does a graduating creative in 2026 need to understand to protect themselves in the world they're entering?
Andrej's institutional role makes him the right voice here. Also directly relevant to Oolite's educational mission.
Follow-up if needed
  • Most university IP policies predate generative AI. Who owns AI-assisted student work when institutional resources are used? Is this being addressed?
  • What's the one piece of legal literacy every creative should have before they take their first client?
→ Somara
You built the Latte Lawyer platform specifically to democratize legal education for creatives. Given everything we've talked about tonight — the Benedict Cork scenario, the speed problem, the epistemic capture question — what does that education look like now, in 2026, versus when you started? What has changed most urgently about what creatives need to know?
Gives Somara a chance to speak from her actual mission. Personal, forward-looking, and connects to the audience's ongoing need beyond tonight.

06

Emergency pivots + contingency questions

Reference only
If conversation stalls or goes off-track: Use these to redirect or restart.
Reset question
Let me bring this back to someone in this room. If you're a visual artist who has never used AI — does anything we've talked about tonight affect you? Or are you safe?
Grounds an abstract conversation back in the room. Almost always provokes a strong answer.
Tension question
I want to put a tension on the table. Some people argue that AI is democratizing creative tools — giving access to people who couldn't afford them before. Others say it's industrializing theft. Are both things true? And does the law help us hold that contradiction or does it have to pick a side?
Injects productive disagreement if the conversation is too aligned.
Somara — personal question
You work with creative entrepreneurs every day. In the last six months — since AI started moving this fast — has the nature of what they're coming to you with changed? What are you seeing on the ground?
Andrej — personal question
You teach law students and you work within an institution. When you're in the classroom talking about AI and IP, what's the question your students ask that you find hardest to answer honestly?
Legal sector recursion — conversation stopper
I want to name something that I find genuinely uncomfortable about this conversation. Both of you use AI tools in your own practice — for research, for drafting, for staying current. Which means both of you are subject to the same epistemic capture dynamic we've been describing for artists. Your legal reasoning, your research patterns, your decision-making process — that's potentially becoming training data for the platforms you use. The legal profession successfully protected its own knowledge assets in the Thomson Reuters case — where a court found that an AI legal research tool trained on Westlaw's headnotes was copyright infringement, not fair use. But are individual lawyers protected from the same extraction that artists face? Or is the profession that's supposed to protect creatives facing the same vulnerability?
The conversation-stopper. Makes the panel self-referential — Somara and Andrej are not outside this problem, they're inside it. Use when the conversation needs a genuine jolt, or as a deliberate penultimate move before the final takeaways. Almost always lands hard.
Follow-up if needed
  • Thomson Reuters won their case — the legal sector protected its own knowledge corpus from AI training. Artists haven't had that win yet. What would it take for the creative sector to have a Thomson Reuters moment?
