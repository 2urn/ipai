Guide 00 · Panel terms

Before we begin

Key terms for tonight's conversation. Starting points, not legal definitions — our attorneys will sharpen, qualify, and argue with all of them.

Glossary
The spectrum of protection
Protected
Fixed, original expression with human authorship
Partial
Human + AI collaboration, documented contributions
Contested
AI-assisted work — courts still deciding "how much"
Unprotected
Purely AI-generated output, style, process, methodology
No recourse
Work spread faster than law can move
Copyright
The automatic legal protection for original creative works fixed in a tangible form — text, images, music, code. In the US, it exists the moment you create something with sufficient human creativity. Registration with the Copyright Office ($65) is not required to own copyright, but is essential for enforcement and damages.
What it doesn't cover: style, ideas, facts, processes, or works created purely by AI.
Human authorship requirement
The principle, now firmly settled in US law, that copyright requires a human creator. Thaler v. Perlmutter: SCOTUS declined to hear the appeal on March 2, 2026, leaving intact the DC Circuit's ruling that AI alone cannot be a copyright author. The key open question: how much human involvement is "enough"?
Denial of cert ≠ a new Supreme Court ruling. It means the lower court decision stands, but SCOTUS has not itself ruled — and could revisit in a future case.
Denial of certiorari
The Supreme Court's refusal to hear a case. It does NOT mean SCOTUS agrees with the lower court — only that they declined to review it this time. The lower court ruling becomes final for that case, but is not binding national Supreme Court precedent. SCOTUS can still take up the same issue in a future case.
Think of it as: the Court said "not this case, not right now" — not "this is settled forever."
Fair use
A legal doctrine allowing limited use of copyrighted material without permission, evaluated case-by-case on four factors: purpose and character of the use, nature of the original work, amount used, and effect on the market for the original. Courts are currently split on whether training AI on copyrighted works qualifies as fair use. Not settled law.
The Copyright Office's Part 3 report (May 2025): "Some uses will qualify as fair use, and some will not." No bright line yet.
Right of publicity
Your legal right to control commercial use of your name, voice, image, and likeness. A state-level right in the US — not federal copyright. Florida has one (Fla. Stat. § 540.08). Increasingly relevant as AI voice and likeness cloning becomes trivial and cheap.
Benedict Cork (Feb 2026): a snippet posted online was run through an AI generator; a completed song appeared on streaming platforms under a different name within days. Cork got it removed — eventually.
Epistemic capture
Not yet a legal category — a framework for understanding what AI tools extract beyond your outputs. When you use an AI platform, your prompts, iterations, decisions, and creative patterns may become training data. Your way of thinking — not just what you make — can be absorbed, modeled, and redistributed without your consent or compensation.
The question isn't just "can someone copy my art?" It's "can someone copy me?"
Training data
The body of existing content used to teach an AI model. Your published work, prompts submitted to AI tools, and interaction patterns may all become training data depending on a platform's terms of service. Whether using copyrighted works as training data constitutes infringement is still being litigated in dozens of active cases.
Terms of service (ToS)
The contract you accept when using any AI tool. Most ToS grant the platform broad rights to use your inputs for training. Many default to inclusion — opt-outs exist but must be actively sought. Reading ToS is now a professional skill for creatives, not a technicality.
Adobe, some Midjourney tiers, and enterprise deployments offer training opt-outs. Free tiers typically do not.
AI music slop / streaming fraud
The industrialized generation and upload of AI music to streaming platforms, either to generate fraudulent royalties or to impersonate artists. Deezer: 50,000 AI-generated tracks uploaded daily = 34% of all new music. A North Carolina man pled guilty in March 2026 to an $8M scheme using AI songs streamed by bots. Independent artists are the primary targets.
DMCA takedown
A legal notice under the Digital Millennium Copyright Act requiring a platform to remove allegedly infringing content. Fast in theory; slow in practice when AI-generated content spreads faster than notices can land, or when content is transformed and redistributed before takedown occurs.
The Anthropic scenario: even a company with legal teams could not contain a leaked code repository before it was refactored in multiple languages and freely redistributed online.

Guide 01

The individual creator

Decision framework for solo artists and creative practitioners using AI tools in their own practice.

Solo practice
Case study: Benedict Cork, February 2026

British singer-songwriter Benedict Cork posted a snippet of his unfinished song "Something Kinda Strange" on TikTok. It received 100,000+ plays. Days later, strangers were texting to congratulate him on the release — which was odd, because he hadn't finished it. Someone had taken his snippet, run it through an AI music generator, added a full second verse and bridge, and published it on streaming platforms under a different name. Cork got it removed after a few days. But the song had already been heard, streamed, and attributed to a stranger. He had almost no immediate legal recourse. "The fact that it's coming up with these things now, when we're only a few years into the AI revolution — what's it gonna be like in 10 years time?"

The scale of the problem (2026)
Streaming fraud
  • 50,000 AI tracks uploaded to Deezer daily — 34% of all new music
  • Sony requested removal of 135,000+ AI impersonation songs in March alone
  • NC man pled guilty: $8M from AI songs streamed by bots
  • Independent artists are primary targets — enough audience, not enough protection
Who gets targeted
  • Low/mid-level artists with devoted followings
  • Dormant artists whose return would spark interest
  • Deceased artists: SOPHIE, Uncle Tupelo
  • Anyone going viral — speed is the weapon
  • "Enough listeners, not enough profile to be protected"
Decision flow: using AI tools in your practice
1
Are you using an AI tool that ingests your prompts, uploads, or style data?
YES → continueNO → standard copyright applies
2
Have you read the tool's Terms of Service regarding training data use?
YES → note what you consented toNO → assume your inputs may train the model risk
3
Is your creative process — prompts, iterations, decisions — being recorded by the platform?
Your methodology may be training data. This is your epistemic exposure.
4
Does the AI output contain significant human creative contribution?
YES → you may claim copyright on your contributionsNO → output likely unprotectable under Thaler (2026)
5
Are you documenting your creative decisions and human input at each stage?
YES → strong copyright positionNO → register your work and start documenting now action
6
Is your voice, likeness, or distinctive style publicly available online?
Your voice/likeness can be cloned. Right of publicity varies by state. Florida: Fla. Stat. § 540.08.
What you can do now
  • Register copyright before publishing AI-assisted work — $65 at copyright.gov
  • Document every iteration: dates, prompts, decisions, edits
  • Check if your work is in training datasets: haveibeentrained.com
  • Submit opt-outs via spawning.ai across platforms
  • Use enterprise or opt-out tiers where possible (Adobe, some Midjourney)
  • Limit high-quality public voice/style samples strategically
What law does NOT protect
  • Purely AI-generated output with no human authorship
  • Your style, aesthetic, or creative approach
  • Your creative process or methodology
  • Prompts submitted to AI tools (unprotectable ideas)
  • Public performances or samples others can capture
  • Voice cloning in most jurisdictions (emerging law)
Key cases + resources
Thaler v. Perlmutter — SCOTUS cert denied March 2, 2026. Human authorship = bedrock requirement of US copyright law.
Allen v. Perlmutter — Pending. Tests how much human involvement is "enough." 600+ iterative prompts may qualify.
NO AI FRAUD Act / NO FAKES Act — Proposed federal legislation targeting AI voice and likeness cloning.
Time Magazine, March 26, 2026 — "AI Slop Is Flooding Streaming." Full Benedict Cork case. time.com

Guide 02

Freelancers + creative teams

Liability cascade and disclosure obligations when AI enters collaborative creative workflows.

Collaboration

The silent liability problem: If a contractor uses AI and doesn't disclose it, the commissioning party may receive work with no valid copyright — and may be unknowingly distributing potentially infringing content. Disclosure isn't just ethical courtesy; it's risk management.

Liability cascade: who owes what to whom

Obligation

Contract clause checklist
  • AI use disclosure clause nowSpecify whether AI tools may be used, which tools are approved, and require written disclosure before delivery.
  • Human authorship warranty nowContractor warrants that deliverables contain sufficient human creative contribution to qualify for copyright protection.
  • Indemnification clause nowContractor indemnifies client against third-party copyright claims arising from undisclosed AI training data use.
  • IP ownership clarity soonDefine who owns AI-assisted outputs, AI-generated drafts, prompts, and iteration files upon project completion.
  • Process documentation requirement soonRequire contractors to maintain records of AI tool use, prompts, and human creative decisions for copyright defense purposes.
  • Methodology protection clause planProhibit use of your distinctive creative process or style data to train third-party AI systems.
Key terms
Work for hire
Work created by an employee in scope of employment, or by a contractor under written agreement in specific categories. Employer/commissioning party owns copyright — but if work lacks human authorship, there may be nothing to own.
Indemnification
A contractual obligation for one party to compensate the other for losses arising from specific acts. For AI work: contractors should indemnify clients for copyright claims arising from their AI tool use.
Warranty of originality
Common contract provision where the creator guarantees the work is original. In the AI era, this must be updated to address AI contributions and copyright eligibility explicitly.
Resources
SAG-AFTRA AI Agreement (2025) — First major union contract with explicit AI protections. A model for other creative sectors.
Illustrators' Partnership AI Clause (2025) — Industry-level model AI provisions for creative contracts.

Guide 03

Client + commercial work

Disclosure requirements, copyright ownership, and liability when delivering AI-assisted creative work to clients.

Commercial
The speed problem

When Anthropic's code repository was leaked and refactored across programming languages before copyright takedown notices could land, the asset was effectively gone. A company with full legal resources could not contain it. If IP law moves in years and AI moves in hours, legal protection is reactive at best. For independent creatives and small studios, the practical strategy must shift: build value around what cannot be extracted — relationship, context, trust, and the irreducibly human.

Before you deliver: disclosure decision tree
1
Does your contract with the client address AI use?
YES → follow those termsNO → disclose proactively before delivery risk
2
Was AI used for ideation/research only, or in the final deliverable?
Ideation use = lower risk. Final output use = must disclose and document human contributions explicitly.
3
Can you warrant that the work contains sufficient human authorship to be copyrightable?
YES + documented → deliver with written disclosure memoNO → do not warrant copyright ownership to client
4
Does the client's industry have specific AI disclosure requirements?
Publishing, advertising, entertainment: emerging sector requirements. Check current guild/union standards before delivery.
5
Did any AI tool you used potentially ingest client confidential material?
Do not input client confidential data into external AI tools without explicit client consent. ToS may not protect client data. risk

Key terms
DMCA takedown
Notice requiring platforms to remove infringing content. Fast in theory; slow in practice when AI content spreads or is transformed faster than notices can land.
Fair use (training)
Courts are split. Copyright Office Part 3 (May 2025): outcomes must be determined case-by-case. Some training = fair use; some = infringement. Not settled law.
Trade secret
Protects confidential business process or method if actively maintained as secret. Inputting your process into an external AI tool may destroy trade secret protection entirely.

Guide 04

Institutional + educational

What creative programs, art schools, and cultural institutions need to teach, implement, and protect — beyond plagiarism.

Institutional

Beyond plagiarism: Current institutional AI policy is largely framed around academic integrity — did the student cheat? The deeper question for creative programs is sovereignty: are we preparing graduates who understand that their creative identity, process, and methodology are now extractable commodities — and who know how to protect them?

What creative programs should teach (beyond plagiarism)
  • Copyright registration as practice habit nowEvery graduating student should know how to register a copyright. US Copyright Office: $65, about one hour. Defense value: essential. Do it before you publish.
  • Reading AI terms of service nowIdentify training data clauses, IP ownership provisions, and opt-out mechanisms in any AI platform ToS before using it professionally.
  • Documenting human creative process nowProcess documentation is now a copyright defense strategy. Save drafts, date iterations, note decisions. This is the evidence for "sufficient human contribution."
  • Understanding epistemic exposure soonWhen students use AI tools, their creative methodology becomes potential training data. The risk isn't just someone copying your output — it's modeling your process.
  • Contract literacy for AI clauses soonStandard freelance contracts must now address AI. Students should recognize and negotiate: disclosure requirements, human authorship warranty, and IP ownership of AI-assisted outputs.
  • Knowing when to ask a lawyer soonBuild a referral ecosystem: legal clinics, IP pro bono resources, bar association artist programs. Creative students often cannot afford legal counsel — institutional programs should bridge this gap.
Institutional policy audit
Area
Current gap
Recommended action
Student IP ownership
Most university IP policies predate generative AI. Who owns AI-assisted student work created using institutional resources?
Update IP policy to explicitly address AI-assisted student work. Default toward student ownership.
Faculty research + AI
Faculty using AI in research may be surrendering institutional IP to platform training data without realizing it.
Establish approved tools list. Require enterprise or private deployment for sensitive research.
Client work in programs
Students doing real client work through institutional programs: undisclosed AI use creates institutional liability.
Require AI disclosure in all student-client agreements. Define the program's warranty position clearly.
Curriculum
Syllabi address plagiarism but not what AI tool data means for student creative identity long-term.
Add AI + IP sovereignty module to all creative programs. Not just plagiarism — creative sovereignty.
